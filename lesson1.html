Lesson 1

Assemble Computer Hardware

This lesson covers the proper assembly of computer hardware, including safety measures. It highlights key components such as the motherboard, processor, RAM, power supply, storage devices, and input/output devices, ensuring compatibility and functionality. The step-by-step process involves installing the motherboard, CPU, RAM, and storage, connecting the power supply and peripherals, and securing cables for a stable system.

Learning Content

Assembling computer hardware is a crucial skill that requires knowledge of different components, their functions, and the proper procedures to ensure system compatibility and stability. Selecting the right components and following safety precautions are essential in building a functional computer. In this lesson, you will learn about the key hardware components, step-by-step assembly, and best practices to avoid damage and ensure optimal performance.

Preparetion And Safety Measures

  1. 1. Unplug Everything – Always disconnect the power before opening a computer.
  2. 2. Ground Yourself – Wear an anti-static wrist strap or touch metal to prevent static damage.
  3. 3. Work on a Clean Surface – Use a non-conductive table to avoid short circuits.
  4. 4. Handle Components Properly – Hold parts by the edges, not the circuits.
  5. 5. Use the Right Tools – A Phillips screwdriver is usually enough; avoid using magnetized tools.
  6. 6. Keep Cables Organized – Prevent clutter and tripping hazards.
  7. 7. Avoid Liquid and Heat Exposure – Keep drinks away and don’t work near direct sunlight.
  8. 8. Check Compatibility – Ensure all parts are compatible before assembly.
  9. 9. Secure Connections – Make sure RAM, CPU, and other components are firmly in place.
  10. 10. Test Before Closing – Power on briefly to check if everything works before sealing the case.

Internal Components

1. Power Supply Unit

The power supply unit in a computer converts the power from the wall outlet to the type of power needed by the computer. A power supply also comes with its own cooling fan. This helps all the internal components to stay cool. when the power supply is subjected to bigger loads.

Examples of PSU Ratings:

• 80 Plus Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum – Efficiency ratings that determine energy consumption.

• Modular vs. Non-Modular – Modular PSUs allow detachable cables for better cable management.

2. Hard Disk Drive

The hard drive is long-term storage, which means the data is still saved even if you turn the computer off or unplug it. A hard drive is found on most computers. It’s usually a mechanical drive that stores all the data. Apart from storing data, it can also be used as a boot drive to run the operating system from it.

4. Random Access Memory

RAM is a data storage device that can provide fast read and write access. RAM is your system's short-term memory. This short- term memory disappears when the computer is turned off.

Examples of RAM Types:

• DDR4/DDR5 – Different generations with varying speeds and efficiency.

• DIMM (Desktop) vs. SO-DIMM (Laptop) – Form factors for different systems.

• ECC RAM – Error-correcting memory used in servers and workstations

Also called a processor, it is sometimes called the brain of the computer, and its job is to carry out commands. It takes information from the RAM and processes it to perform the tasks required from the computer.

• Cores and Threads – Determines multitasking capabilities and speed.
